\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Motivation}

\textbf{Demographic Winter} (i.e., \textit{declining birth rates coupled with aging population}) has become a salient issue among national governments and public opinions due to its potential  negative effect on:
\medskip

\begin{itemize}
    \item Innovation, productivity and  economic growth slowdown
    \medskip
    \item Welfare system sustainability
    \medskip
    \item Cultural and societal shifts 
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Motivation}
Western democracies have started to address some of its economic aspects:
\medskip
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{The cost of childbearing}: provision of financial and/or fiscal incentives depending on the number of children (e.g., Family Act 2020, Social Security Contribution Exemption with 2+ children in Budget Law 2024)
\medskip
\item \textbf{Work-life balance}: maternity/paternity leaves, provision of affordable kindergartens (e.g., Kindergarten Bonus in Budget Law 2024) 
\end{itemize}
\medskip
Part of the electorate view demographic winter as primarily due to the change in gender roles within households, advocating for a return to a "traditional family" via:
\medskip

\begin{itemize}
\item  \textbf{Indirect intervention by rising the economic costs} of alternative models (e.g., the use of the "family quotient" instead of separated taxation) 
    \medskip

    \item \textbf{Direct intervention on beliefs} (e.g., propaganda, indoctrination)

    
\end{itemize}


\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{What We May Find}

In principle, the impact of indoctrination on fertility is not obvious \textit{a priori}:
\medskip
\begin{itemize}
    \item Indoctrination works $\rightarrow$ fertility $\uparrow$
    \medskip
    
    \item Indoctrination does not work $\rightarrow$ fertility $=$
    \medskip
    
    \item Indoctrination creates backlash $\rightarrow$ fertility $\downarrow$
    \medskip
    
\end{itemize}
\end{frame} 


\begin{frame}
\frametitle{What We Find}
\begin{itemize}
    \item At the aggregate level, fertility dropped significantly in places with a bigger share of female population potentially exposed to the reform.
\medskip
 \item At the individual level,   women belonging to birth cohorts affected by the reform do not show a different probability to become mothers with respect to women who are not affected by that. Nonetheless, the former have significantly fewer children than the latter.
\medskip
    \item The drop in fertility outcomes for treated cohorts is associated with a shift in (primary and secondary) beliefs possibly affected by the reform, eventually back-lashing the indoctrination perpetrated by the regime.

\end{itemize}


\end{frame}

\section{Background}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Historical Background}
\begin{itemize}
\item Civil War (1936-1939), followed by a military dictatorship (1939-1975)
\item Interest on demographic policies
\begin{itemize}
    \item {\tiny {\color{gray}Ramiro Ledesma- founder of the political party \textit{JONS}: "We can only seriously speak of Spain's superiority when it becomes attainable through a population that has at least doubled"}}

    \item {\tiny {\color{gray}Maternity was described as an \textit{"ordinary way of life to reach perfection"} and represented the feminine goal: \textit{"the feminine profession should be oriented towards family life: it belongs to the very essence of a woman to be always and everywhere a mother, to devote and sacrifice herself, and to care attentively for others"} }}
\end{itemize}
\item Primary Education Law under Franco rule \textit{(BOE 1945)}
    \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Religion} became compulsory at all educational levels
    \item Strong \textbf{National identity}
    \item \textbf{Strict control} of all educational materials and contents (Canales, 2021)
    \item  \textbf{Prohibition} of regional languages and mandatory use of the Spanish language
    \item \textbf{Teachers} were subordinated to the national interest
    \item \textbf{Gender-specific} education
    \begin{itemize}
    \item Separate classes for boys and girls
     
     \item Different education for girls to prepare for domestic life and motherhood
\end{itemize}
     \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Outcome Variable}
\label{empirical}
\textbf{Measuring Fertility}:
\medskip
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Total Fertility Rates} are the most suited to make comparisons within and between units. Unfortunately, we do not have the number of childbirths by women's age groups. 
    \medskip
    \item \textbf{What we have:} \textit{Total alive births}. 
    \begin{itemize}

\medskip 
    \item Once we control for the number of women in fertile age, i.e., aged 15-44 in each census decade, it is like using the \textit{General Fertility Rates}  
    \medskip
    \item GFRs are a legitimate measure of fertility as long as the  (relative) size and the composition of the fertile age group remain constant in time and space \hyperlink{stability_age}{\beamerbutton{Stability of age groups' size}}

    \end{itemize}

    \medskip
    
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\item We can compare the general fertility rate within and between units where the share of population exposed to the reform is higher or lower
%\medskip 
%\item Thus, we cannot distinguish the effect of the reform from the natural impact on fertility to have a smaller fertile group within the population
\end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
\medskip
\textbf{Solution:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item On the one hand, the generational turnover in the female fertile age group allows us to roughly predict when the largest impact on general fertility rates should occur.
\medskip
\item On the other hand, by using individual-level fertility data, we estimate the effect of the reform by exploiting variation in reform exposure across birth cohorts

\end{itemize}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Conclusion}
\begin{itemize}
\item This study contributes to the  literature on the demographic effects of political indoctrination and education policies
\medskip

    \item The Francoist regime's primary education reform led to significant demographic changes
    \medskip

\item The policy, which included indoctrination through education, generated unintended effects on the fertility rates in Spain
\medskip

\item Analysis of beliefs shows lasting hostility in the following decades by the cohorts exposed to Franco's education reforms towards the values of religion, family and gender roles promoted by the regime
\medskip

\item This hostility was likely a key driver of the fertility backlash documented here
\item Policy message: avoid coercive narratives. Promoting gender equity may be more effective
\end{itemize}
