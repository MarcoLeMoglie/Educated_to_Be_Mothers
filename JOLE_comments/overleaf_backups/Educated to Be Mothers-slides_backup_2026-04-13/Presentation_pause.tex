\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Motivation}

\textbf{Demographic Winter} (i.e., \textit{declining birth rates coupled with aging population}) has become a salient issue among national governments and public opinions due to its potential  negative effect on:
\medskip
\pause
\begin{itemize}
    \item Innovation, productivity and  economic growth slowdown
    \medskip\pause
    \item Welfare system sustainability
    \medskip\pause
    \item Cultural and societal shifts (backward?)
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Motivation}
Western democracies have started to address some of its economic aspects:
\medskip
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{The cost of childbearing}: provision of financial and/or fiscal incentives depending on the number of children (e.g., Family Act 2020, Social Security Contribution Exemption with 2+ children in Budget Law 2024)
\medskip\pause
\item \textbf{Work-life balance}: maternity/paternity leaves, provision of affordable kindergartens (e.g., Kindergarten Bonus in Budget Law 2024) 
\end{itemize}
\medskip\pause
Part of the electorate view demographic winter as primarily due to the change in gender roles within households, advocating for a return to a "traditional family" via:
\medskip\pause

\begin{itemize}
\item  \textbf{Indirect intervention by rising the economic costs} of alternative models (e.g., the use of the "family quotient" instead of separated taxation) \pause
    \medskip

    \item \textbf{Direct intervention on beliefs} (e.g., propaganda, indoctrination)

    
\end{itemize}


\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{What We May Find}

In principle, the impact of indoctrination on fertility is not obvious \textit{a priori}:
\medskip\pause
\begin{itemize}
    \item Indoctrination works $\rightarrow$ fertility $\uparrow$
    \medskip
    \pause
    \item Indoctrination does not work $\rightarrow$ fertility $=$
    \medskip
    \pause
    \item Indoctrination creates backlash $\rightarrow$ fertility $\downarrow$
    \medskip
    
\end{itemize}
\end{frame} 


\begin{frame}
\frametitle{What We Find}
\begin{itemize}
    \item At the aggregate level, fertility dropped significantly in places with a bigger share of female population potentially exposed to the reform.
\medskip\pause
 \item At the individual level,   women belonging to birth cohorts affected by the reform do not show a different probability to become mothers with respect to women who are not affected by that. Nonetheless, the former have in general fewer children than the latter.
\medskip\pause
    \item The drop in fertility outcomes for treated cohorts is associated with a shift in (primary and secondary) beliefs possibly affected by the reform, eventually back-lashing the indoctrination perpetrated by the regime.

\end{itemize}


\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[label=empirical]
\frametitle{Outcome Variable}

\textbf{Measuring Fertility}:
\medskip
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Total Fertility Rates} are the most suited to make comparisons within and between units. Unfortunately, we do not have the number of childbirths by women's age groups. 
    \medskip\pause
    \item \textbf{Solution:} \textit{General Fertility Rates}. 
    \begin{itemize}

\medskip 
    \item Number of children in each capital/rest of province divided by number of women in fertile age, i.e., aged 15-44, in 1940 
    \medskip\pause
    \item Legitimate measure as long as the relative size (and composition) of fertile age groups remains constant in time and space \hyperlink{stability_age}{\beamerbutton{Stability of age groups' size}}
    \medskip\pause
    \item To definitively overcome the problem we fix the denominator of the rate in a specific year (i.e., 1940) and we test that it is not statistically different between treated and control units.
    \end{itemize}

    \medskip
    
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[label=empirical2]
\frametitle{Identification}


\textbf{Isolating the effect of the reform:}
\medskip
\begin{itemize}
\item Lack of data on fertility rates by age group does not allow us to directly estimate the effect of the reform on fertility. 
\begin{itemize}
\pause
\medskip
\item We cannot directly compare, within and between units, the fertility rate of age groups exposed or not to the reform over time 
\medskip\pause
\item We can compare the general fertility rate within and between units where the share of population exposed to the reform is higher or lower
%\medskip 
%\item Thus, we cannot distinguish the effect of the reform from the natural impact on fertility to have a smaller fertile group within the population
\end{itemize}
\medskip \pause
\item \textbf{Solution}: Exploiting the cleansing of teachers as a further source of variation in the intensity of the reform within the same group of cohorts and between units 
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

y_{i} =\beta_0+  \beta_1  \textit{Cohorts 1931-1940}_i + \beta_2 \textit{Cohorts 1941-1970}_i+\Gamma_i +\epsilon_{i}
%\end{split}
\end{equation}
\smallskip\pause
\begin{itemize}
    \item $y_i$ are respondent \textit{i}'s  demographics and beliefs about religion, family and gender roles
    \medskip\pause
    \item $\textit{Cohorts 1931-1940}_i$ is a dummy equal to 1 for cohorts only partially exposed to the reform 
    \medskip
    \item  $\textit{Cohorts 1941-1970}_i$ is a dummy equal to 1 for cohorts fully exposed to the reform
    \medskip\pause
    \item $\Gamma_i$ are fixed effects at the municipality level
    \medskip\pause
    \item $\epsilon_i$ are heteroskedastic-robust standard errors clustered at the locality level
    \medskip\pause
    \item Some outcome variables (i.e., kids, ideology and religion) are observed in multiple waves, allowing us to control for year fixed effects
\end{itemize}


\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Conclusion}
\begin{itemize}
\item This study contributes to the  literature on the demographic effects of political indoctrination and education policies
\medskip
\pause
    \item The Francoist regime's primary education reform led to significant demographic changes
    \medskip
\pause
\item The policy, which included indoctrination through education, generated unintended effects on the fertility and marriage rates in Spain
\medskip
\pause
\item Analysis of beliefs shows lasting hostility in the following decades by the cohorts exposed to Franco's education reforms towards the values of religion, family and gender roles promoted by the regime
\medskip
\pause
\item This hostility was likely a key driver of the fertility backlash documented here
\end{itemize}
