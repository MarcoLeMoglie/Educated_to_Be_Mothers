Comment 2



CLUSTERING LEVEL FOR STANDARD ERRORS



"Standard errors are heteroskedasticity-robust and clustered at the level of birth cohort-survey year. ${ }^{20}$  

${ }^{20}$ The reason for this is twofold. First, clustering only by birth cohort, the level at which treatment is assigned, would result in a small number of clusters (i.e. 31), which can lead to underestimated standard errors. Second, and more importantly, clustering by both birth cohort and survey year better accounts for the residual correlation among respondents who share both the timing of exposure and the survey wave in which they were interviewed."



Message: Treatment varies only by birth cohort, yet the paper clusters standard errors by the finer “birth cohort × survey year” cell.  This breaks the correlation between respondents from the same cohort interviewed in different waves and can understate sampling variability if cohort-level shocks persist across waves.  A more defensible approach is to keep clustering at the cohort level and rely on a small-cluster-robust procedure (e.g. wild cluster bootstrap) or to use two-way clustering by cohort and survey year.  Recomputing inference with one of these methods would strengthen the credibility of the reported p-values.

DISTINCTION BETWEEN "POLITICAL" AND "CULTURAL" BACKLASH



"Being exposed to an authoritarian far-right regime that promoted traditional family values through education may have triggered a long-term ideological backlash among affected women, fostering more progressive or left-leaning attitudes... Message: It is not obvious why a change in attitudes toward abortion, contraception, or household gender roles is categorized as a “cultural backlash,” while a change in party vote share is the sole test of a “political identity” backlash.  Because these moral issues were heavily politicised in post-Franco Spain, a reader may reasonably assume they belong to the same ideological channel.  The current wording—especially the claim that the null result on right-wing voting “rules out most of the channels discussed above”—risks overstating what a single partisan proxy can exclude and may obscure the intended distinction between partisan realignment and shifts in domain-specific social norms.  Clarifying why the paper treats vote choice and the belief measures as separate mechanisms, and tempering the language that dismisses the former, would make the argument easier to follow.
